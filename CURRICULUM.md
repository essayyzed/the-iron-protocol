# üéì CS/SE/IT 4-Year Roadmap ‚Äì Student Portal

> **Callout:** This portal is your guide to becoming **industry-ready** by the end of your degree. It combines university courses, practical skills, projects, and career prep into one clear roadmap.

---

## üß≠ What This Is

This portal gives you a **semester-by-semester roadmap** for:

- BS **Computer Science**
- BS **Software Engineering**
- BS **Information Technology**
- And related degrees (AI / Data Science / Cybersecurity), with specialization later.

It is based on:

- Industry experience.
- Realistic hiring expectations.
- A "breadth-first, specialization-later" philosophy.
- The learning principles explained in the video series you shared.

## üë• Who This Is For

- Students in **any year** (1st to 4th).
- Students who want to be **employable**, not just degree-holders.
- Students willing to put in **consistent daily effort**.

## üèó Core Principles of This Roadmap

1. **Breadth first, specialization later**
   Build strong fundamentals in Years 1‚Äì2. Explore multiple fields in Year 2. Specialize in Year 3‚Äì4.
2. **Coding is non-negotiable**
   No matter your title (CS, SE, AI, DS, Security), you must be able to write and understand code.
3. **Problem-solving > tools**
   Languages and frameworks change. The ability to think clearly and break down problems does not.
4. **Attention is your superpower**
   Protect your focus from Shorts, Reels, TikTok, and endless scrolling. Deep work beats scattered attention.
5. **Connected learning**
   Don't memorize isolated facts. Always link new concepts to things you already know.
6. **AI is a tool, not your brain**
   Use AI (ChatGPT, Copilot, etc.) to **understand and verify**, not to copy answers blindly.
7. **Portfolio > GPA in industry**
   A strong website, GitHub, and projects often matter more than your exact CGPA in job interviews.

# üü¶ Year 1 ‚Äì Foundations & Practical Skills

> **Year 1 Goal:** Build solid foundations in problem-solving, typing, Linux, and web development. Start your journey as a real builder, not a passive student.

## üß≠ Year 1 Overview

- Learn **how to learn**.
- Build a **problem-solving mindset**.
- Start using **Linux** and the **terminal**.
- Learn **basic programming**.
- Build your **first websites and apps**.
- Create your **GitHub account**.

## üìå Semester 1 ‚Äì Foundations & Habits

### üéØ Goals

- Build a problem-solving mindset.
- Establish core habits: typing, Linux, asking good questions.
- Learn basics of computing and programming.

### üìö University Courses (Typical)

- Introduction to Computing.
- Programming Fundamentals (C/C++/Python/Java).
- Calculus I or Discrete Mathematics I.
- English/Communication.

### üõ† Parallel Track (Self-Directed)

#### Daily Habits

- **15 minutes:** Touch typing practice (use TypingClub, Keybr, or Monkeytype).
- Target: 35+ WPM by end of semester.

- **30‚Äì60 minutes:** Use Linux as your primary OS.
- Install Ubuntu/Fedora/Pop!\_OS (dual boot or primary).
- Do all your coursework in Linux.
- Learn terminal basics: `ls`, `cd`, `cp`, `mv`, `mkdir`, `rm`, `grep`, `find`, `man`.

#### Weekly Tasks

- Watch 1‚Äì2 videos from:
- ["How to Approach Learning" playlist](https://www.youtube.com/@recluze/playlists) - Recluze
- ["Introduction to Computing" playlist](https://www.youtube.com/@recluze/playlists) - Recluze
- [CS50 lectures](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLhQjrBD2T380F_inVRXMIHCqLaNUd7bN4) - Harvard

- Write at least **1 good question per week** (following the protocol: Write ‚Üí Narrow ‚Üí Search ‚Üí Ask).
- Join or form a **goal-oriented study group** (3‚Äì5 students with similar ambitions).

### üì¶ Mini Projects (End of Semester Targets)

1. **CLI Calculator** (simple command-line calculator in your programming language).
2. **Text-based game** (e.g., number guessing, tic-tac-toe, simple adventure).
3. **Linux setup documentation** (write a blog post or doc about your Linux setup and what you learned).

### ‚úÖ Semester 1 Checklist

- [ ] I can type at **35+ WPM**.
- [ ] I use **Linux** comfortably for basic tasks.
- [ ] I have completed at least **2 small coding projects**.
- [ ] I wrote and asked at least **4 good questions**.
- [ ] I formed or joined a **goal-oriented study group**.

### ‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes

- ‚ùå Skipping typing practice ("I'll do it later").
- ‚ùå Staying in Windows/Mac comfort zone (you won't learn problem-solving).
- ‚ùå Copying code without understanding.
- ‚ùå Not asking questions (fear of looking dumb).

## üìå Semester 2 ‚Äì Web Development "Confidence Builder"

### üéØ Goals

- Give yourself **visible results** quickly (websites, small apps).
- Build basic frontend and simple backend skills.
- Start using Git and GitHub.

### üìö University Courses (Typical)

- Programming II / Object-Oriented Programming.
- Data Structures (intro).
- Calculus II or Discrete Mathematics II.
- Physics or other general education.

### üß≠ Web Dev Learning Path (16 Weeks)

Dedicate **2‚Äì2.5 hours per day** to web development.

#### Phase 1: Frontend Basics (Weeks 1‚Äì4)

1. HTML (structure, semantic tags).
2. CSS (styling, box model, flexbox, grid).
3. Bootstrap (responsive design, components).

#### Phase 2: JavaScript (Weeks 5‚Äì8)

4. JavaScript basics (variables, functions, loops, conditionals).
5. ES6 features (arrow functions, let/const, template literals, destructuring).
6. DOM manipulation (selecting elements, event listeners, dynamic content).

#### Phase 3: Backend Basics (Weeks 9‚Äì12)

7. Node.js (runtime, npm, modules).
8. Express.js (routing, middleware, serving pages).
9. MongoDB (NoSQL basics, CRUD operations).

#### Phase 4: Integration (Weeks 13‚Äì16)

10. Build a full-stack CRUD app (e.g., Notes app, Todo app, Simple blog).
11. Git & GitHub (version control, commits, push/pull, README).
12. Deploy your app (Heroku, Render, Vercel, or Netlify).

### üì¶ Projects (End of Semester Targets)

1. **Static portfolio website** (HTML/CSS/Bootstrap) ‚Äì host on GitHub Pages.
2. **Dynamic CRUD app** (Node.js + Express + MongoDB) ‚Äì e.g., Notes app, Todo list, or simple blog.
3. **GitHub profile** with at least 2 active repositories.

### ‚úÖ Semester 2 Checklist

- [ ] I understand HTML/CSS/Bootstrap layout.
- [ ] I can write JS for basic interactivity.
- [ ] I can build a simple CRUD app with Node/Express/Mongo.
- [ ] I have a **GitHub profile** with real code.
- [ ] I have **deployed** at least one project.

### ‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes

- ‚ùå Starting with PHP (outdated approach; start with modern JS stack).
- ‚ùå Copy-pasting code (type it out to build muscle memory).
- ‚ùå Skipping Git (you'll regret it later).
- ‚ùå Not deploying (if it's not live, it doesn't feel real).

## ‚òÄÔ∏è Summer Break 1 ‚Äì "Missing Semester" (System Administration)

### üéØ Goals

- Acquire hands-on systems knowledge that universities rarely teach.
- Learn to deploy and manage real servers.
- Understand how the internet actually works.

### üìÖ 8‚Äì10 Week Plan

#### Week 1‚Äì2: Linux Server Basics

- Rent a cheap VPS (DigitalOcean, Linode, Vultr, AWS Lightsail ‚Äì $5‚Äì10/month).
- SSH into your server.
- User management, permissions, file system.
- Install and configure a web server (Nginx or Apache).
- Host a simple static site.

#### Week 3‚Äì4: Docker Basics

- What are containers?
- Install Docker.
- Run pre-built containers (Nginx, MySQL, etc.).
- Write a simple Dockerfile.
- Docker Compose basics.

#### Week 5‚Äì6: Automation & Scripting

- Bash scripting (loops, conditionals, functions).
- Automate a task (e.g., backup script, log rotation).
- Optional: Intro to Puppet or Ansible.

#### Week 7‚Äì8: Advanced Topics (choose 1‚Äì2)

- **VoIP:** Set up Asterisk (if interested in telecom).
- **Security:** SSH keys, firewall (ufw/iptables), fail2ban.
- **Monitoring:** Set up basic monitoring (htop, netdata, or Grafana).

#### Week 9‚Äì10: Integration Project

- Deploy your Semester 2 web app on your VPS using Docker.
- Set up a domain name (optional but recommended).
- Write documentation (blog post or README).

### üì¶ Outputs

- ‚úÖ Personal website or app hosted on a VPS you control.
- ‚úÖ At least one service containerized with Docker.
- ‚úÖ Documented setup (blog post, GitHub README, or personal notes).
- ‚úÖ Comfort with SSH, Linux server management, and basic DevOps.

### üîó Resources

- MIT's "Missing Semester" course (online, free).
- DigitalOcean tutorials (excellent step-by-step guides).
- Docker documentation.
- YouTube tutorials (search for specific tools).

## üìå Semester 3 ‚Äì Core CS I (Data Structures & Algorithms)

### üéØ Goals

- Solidify understanding of data structures and algorithms.
- Start understanding how computers work internally.
- Build problem-solving muscle.

### üìö University Courses (Typical)

- Data Structures & Algorithms.
- Computer Organization / Architecture.
- Discrete Mathematics II or Logic.
- Elective or general education.

### üõ† Parallel Track (Self-Directed)

#### Weekly Practice

- Solve **2‚Äì3 coding problems** per week (LeetCode Easy, HackerRank, Codeforces).
- Focus on:
- Arrays, strings.
- Linked lists.
- Stacks, queues.
- Basic trees (binary trees, BST).
- Recursion.
- Time/space complexity analysis.

#### Conceptual Study

- Watch OS basics from playlists (processes, memory, scheduling ‚Äì conceptual level for now).
- Start connecting: "How does a linked list work in memory?" "What does the OS do when I allocate memory?"

### üì¶ Projects (End of Semester Targets)

1. **Custom data structure implementation** (e.g., implement a stack, queue, or linked list from scratch).
2. **Mini-project using DS** (e.g., simple library management system, contact manager, or expression evaluator).

### ‚úÖ Semester 3 Checklist

- [ ] Comfort with arrays, linked lists, stacks, queues, basic trees.
- [ ] Can analyze time complexity of simple algorithms.
- [ ] Completed 1 DS/Algo mini-project.
- [ ] Solved 20‚Äì30 coding problems.

### ‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes

- ‚ùå Memorizing solutions (understand the pattern, not the code).
- ‚ùå Skipping complexity analysis (it's critical for interviews).
- ‚ùå Only doing theory (implement everything in code).

## üìå Semester 4 ‚Äì Core CS II (OS, Networks, Databases)

### üéØ Goals

- Understand operating systems and networking with hands-on labs.
- Connect theory to your previous system admin experience.
- Build database-backed applications.

### üìö University Courses (Typical)

- Operating Systems.
- Computer Networks.
- Database Systems.
- Elective.

### üõ† Parallel Track (Self-Directed)

#### OS Labs (hands-on)

- **Processes:** Write programs that fork, exec, wait.
- **Pipes:** Inter-process communication.
- **Threads:** Multi-threaded programs, mutexes, race conditions.
- **Signals:** Handle signals in C/C++.

#### Networking Labs (hands-on)

- **Socket programming:** Write a simple TCP client and server.
- **HTTP basics:** Understand requests/responses.
- **Packet analysis:** Use Wireshark to inspect traffic.

#### Database Practice

- Design a small database schema (e.g., for a library, e-commerce, or school).
- Write SQL queries (SELECT, JOIN, GROUP BY, etc.).
- Build a simple app that uses a database (can extend your Semester 2 web app).

### üì¶ Projects (End of Semester Targets)

1. **Networking project:** Simple chat server (TCP sockets).
2. **OS project:** Multi-threaded application (e.g., producer-consumer, web server).
3. **Database project:** CRUD app with SQL database (e.g., inventory system, student records).

### ‚úÖ Semester 4 Checklist

- [ ] Understanding of processes, threads, concurrency.
- [ ] 1 networking project (socket programming).
- [ ] 1 OS-related project (threads/processes).
- [ ] 1 database-backed application.

### ‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes

- ‚ùå Only studying theory (OS/Networks require hands-on practice).
- ‚ùå Skipping labs (they're where real learning happens).
- ‚ùå Not connecting to previous knowledge (link back to Docker, servers, web dev).

# üß© Projects & Portfolio

## üß© Why Projects Matter

In industry hiring:

- **Projects demonstrate ability** (grades only show you can pass exams).
- **Portfolio is your proof** (anyone can claim skills on a CV).
- **GitHub shows your work ethic** (consistent commits signal discipline).

## üìÜ Recommended Projects by Year

### Year 1

- Static portfolio website (HTML/CSS).
- Dynamic CRUD app (Node.js + MongoDB or similar).
- Linux server setup (documented).

### Year 2

- Networking project (socket programming).
- OS project (multi-threading).
- Database-backed app (SQL).
- 5 "Hello World" exploration projects (ML, security, mobile, cloud, data).

### Year 3

- 1 significant specialization project (6‚Äì12 months).
- 1 team project with CI/CD.
- Internship work (if applicable).

### Year 4

- Final Year Project (your flagship).
- 1‚Äì2 smaller projects to fill gaps in portfolio.

## üåê Portfolio Website Structure

Your personal website should have:

1. **Home/About:** Who you are, what you do, what you're interested in.
2. **Projects:** 5‚Äì10 best projects with:

- Title.
- Description (problem, solution, tech stack).
- Screenshots or demo video.
- GitHub link.
- Live demo link (if applicable).

3. **Skills:** List of technologies you're comfortable with.
4. **Blog (optional but recommended):** Write about what you're learning.
5. **Contact:** Email, LinkedIn, GitHub.

## üêô GitHub Best Practices

- **Pin your best repos** (top 6 are visible on your profile).
- **Write good READMEs:**
- What the project does.
- How to install/run it.
- Screenshots.
- Tech stack.
- Your role (if team project).

- **Commit regularly** (even small commits show consistency).
- **Use meaningful commit messages** (not "fixed bug" or "update").
- **Archive or delete weak repos** (quality > quantity).

# üíº Career Preparation

## üí≠ The Two Objectives

1. **Earn well** (money follows value creation).
2. **Enjoy your work** (life is too short for misery).

## üß† Mindset Shift: Value Creation, Not Begging

**Wrong mindset:** "Please hire me, I need a job, I'll take any salary"
**Right mindset:** "I can solve X problem for you, here's proof (portfolio), let's discuss how I can add value"

## üéØ The "You Attitude"

Always frame your CV, cover letter, and interviews around:

- What you can do for the employer.
- What value you bring.
- What problems you can solve.

Not:

- What you want to learn.
- What you need.
- What you hope to gain.

## üìÑ CV Best Practices

- **Length:** 1‚Äì2 pages (1 page for fresh graduates).
- **Remove weak "Objective" section** (replace with a strong summary if needed).
- **Focus on impact:**
- Bad: "Worked on a web app"
- Good: "Built a CRUD web app serving 100+ users, reducing manual data entry time by 50%".

- **Quantify where possible:** Users, performance improvements, lines of code, team size, etc.
- **Tech stack:** List technologies used for each project.
- **No lies:** Everything on your CV should be defensible in an interview.

## üåê Website as Differentiator

Most candidates don't have a personal website. If you do, you're already in the top 10%.

## üêô GitHub as Signal

Employers will check your GitHub. Make sure it shows:

- Consistent activity (green squares).
- Quality projects (not just forks).
- Good documentation.

## üíº LinkedIn Optimization

- Professional photo.
- Strong headline (not just "Student at X University").
- Detailed experience section (treat it like your CV).
- Skills section (get endorsements from peers).
- Recommendations (ask professors, internship supervisors).

## üéØ Application Strategy

- **Apply broadly:** Don't wait for "perfect" match. Apply if you're 60%+ qualified.
- **Volume matters:** 50‚Äì100+ applications is normal.
- **Learn fast:** If a job requires a skill you don't have, learn it in 2‚Äì3 days before the interview.
- **Follow up:** Send a polite follow-up email 1 week after applying.

## üß™ Interview Prep

### Technical Interviews

- Practice coding problems (LeetCode, HackerRank).
- Focus on Medium difficulty.
- Understand patterns (two pointers, sliding window, BFS/DFS, dynamic programming).
- Practice explaining your thought process out loud.

### Behavioral Interviews

- Prepare stories for common questions ("Tell me about a time you failed", "Describe a conflict in a team").
- Use the **STAR method** (Situation, Task, Action, Result).
- Research the company before the interview.
